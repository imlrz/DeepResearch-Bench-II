Critiques of Traditional Methods

Traditional approaches to writing Chinese literary history, as critiqued by Stephen Owen and others, are often rooted in 19th-century European nationalism and historicism. These methods emphasize teleological narratives—stories of origin, development, maturity, and decline—that construct national identity through unified ethnic vernaculars. Core principles include chronological ordering, encyclopedic listings of authors and works, and a focus on internal evolution (autonomy) or external socio-historical factors (heteronomy, often Marxist-influenced). Problems arise from oversimplification, ignoring synchronic diversity, institutional processes, and the fluidity of texts, leading to distorted representations. For instance, they mechanically divide literature by genres and periods, prioritizing canonical highlights without contextual integration.tsla.researchcommons.org

New Theories Proposed

To counter these, Owen introduces subversive principles like the "history of literary culture," which reconstructs literary historiography as "histories of histories" (史中之史)—tracing production, circulation, reception, and canonization within discursive communities rather than linear timelines. This non-teleological model uses synchronic cross-sections and hierarchical structures to highlight heterogeneity. A key concept is the "Cultural Tang" (650–1020), bounded by cultural shifts rather than dynasties, emphasizing transitions from court-centered to elite-possessed literature. These ideas promote a dynamic view of literature as social practice, integrating textuality of history and historicity of texts.tsla.researchcommons.org

Influence of New Historicism

New Historicism, particularly Stephen Greenblatt's theories, influences the book by rejecting deterministic contextualism and examining texts within networks of linguistic, socio-political, and institutional elements. Core concepts like "textual historicity" (texts shaped by historical moments) and "historical textuality" (history influenced by literary forms) shift the perspective from pure "literature" to "literary culture." This manifests in analyzing production contexts, such as patronage and education, and reconstructing canons through institutions, as seen in non-linear narratives and cultural materialism. The approach aligns with Greenblatt's cultural poetics, fostering plural explanations over single-line schemas.tsla.researchcommons.org

Redefining "Chineseness"

The book redefines "Chinese literature" and "Chineseness" by moving from nation-state boundaries to "discursive communities"—groups sharing literary values across regions. This impacts language (from ornate court styles to plain, individualized diction) and geography (decentralizing from centers like Chang'an to peripheries). A specific example is Chapter IV, "The Cultural Tang," which traces temple-visiting poems from courtly conventions to decentralized, ascetic expressions in late Tang circles. This broadens the scope to include multicultural elements, challenging Sinocentric canons.tsla.researchcommons.org

The Theoretical Innovations in The Cambridge History of Chinese Literature

The Cambridge History of Chinese Literature (2010), co-edited by Kang-i Sun Chang and Stephen Owen, represents a significant departure from conventional historiography in Chinese literature. This two-volume work, covering from ancient times to the early 21st century, seeks to reconstruct literary narratives through a lens that integrates cultural, social, and institutional contexts. Rather than evaluating its merits or limitations, this report focuses on elucidating the core theoretical shifts it embodies, drawing from Owen's editorial vision and contributions from various scholars. These innovations address longstanding issues in literary historiography while proposing alternative frameworks influenced by contemporary critical theories.

1. Critiques of Traditional Methods

Traditional methods in Chinese literary history writing, as articulated by Owen, are deeply indebted to 19th-century European models of nationalism and historicism, which emerged alongside the formation of nation-states. These approaches often construct teleological narratives—linear stories of literary evolution from origin through development, maturity, and potential decline—that serve to unify national identity through an ethnic vernacular, excluding elements deemed non-national (e.g., Latin texts in Italian histories or pre-modern works like Beowulf in English literary canons). Core principles underpinning these methods include:tsla.researchcommons.orgcuhk.edu.hk

  • Chronological and Encyclopedic Structuring: Histories are organized by dynasties or periods, presenting exhaustive lists of authors, titles, and genres, often reducing complex cultural phenomena to simplified timelines.lhp.sdu.edu.cn
  • Autonomy and Heteronomy: Emphasis on internal linguistic or generic evolution (autonomy) or external socio-historical influences (heteronomy, frequently Marxist), leading to deterministic schemas that overlook synchronic diversity and institutional dynamics.sciencedirect.com
  • Nationalistic Ideology: Rooted in ideologies of state or nation, these narratives prioritize canonical highlights, fostering misinterpretations by ignoring the fluidity of texts, losses in transmission, and later reconstructions.sciencedirect.com

Such methods, introduced to China via Japanese sinologists and early Western works like Herbert A. Giles's A History of Chinese Literature (1901), result in oversimplifications, anachronisms, and a failure to account for broader historical contexts, such as manuscript cultures or peripheral influences. For example, traditional views stereotype Eastern Jin poetry as solely "arcane discourse," neglecting diverse topics and societal integrations.tsla.researchcommons.orglhp.sdu.edu.cn

2. New Theories Proposed

In response, Owen advocates for disruptive principles that reframe literary history as a "history of literary culture," emphasizing the reconstruction of historiography itself through "histories of histories" (史中之史)—a meta-historical approach tracing how literary narratives have been shaped over time. This involves non-linear, hierarchical structures with synchronic "cross-sections" to capture heterogeneity, moving away from teleological models toward dynamic systems of cultural production. Core ideas include:tsla.researchcommons.orgcuhk.edu.hk

  • History of Literary Culture: Literature as integrated with cultural practices, focusing on production (e.g., patronage), circulation, reception, and canonization within communities, rather than isolated texts.lhp.sdu.edu.cn
  • Cultural Tang: A conceptual period (650–1020) defined by cultural transitions, not dynasties, highlighting shifts from court-centered to elite-driven literature, proto-professionalism, and interrelationships in genres like prose narratives post-790s.sciencedirect.com
  • Three-Dimensional Research: Posing questions on text origins, canon selection, characteristics, and intertextual relations to create multi-level narratives.sciencedirect.com

These principles, akin to shifting from "Newtonian" to "quantum" physics in historiography, integrate the textuality of history (literature shaping social life) and historicity of texts (historical influences on literature). For instance, Xiaofei Tian's chapter on Eastern Jin to early Tang restores texts to production contexts, revealing interconnected features across boundaries.tsla.researchcommons.orglhp.sdu.edu.cn

3. Influence of New Historicism

New Historicism, emerging in the 1980s and popularized in the 1990s, profoundly shapes the book's methodology, drawing from Stephen Greenblatt's emphasis on cultural poetics and the reciprocal relations between texts and contexts. Rejecting 19th-century determinism (e.g., Taine's race-milieu-moment), it treats literature within networks of power, institutions, and discourses, influenced by postmodernism and post-structuralism. Key concepts applied include:tsla.researchcommons.orgcuhk.edu.hk

  • Textual Historicity: Texts as products of specific historical moments, examined through production elements like education and exams (e.g., jinshi addition of poetry in 679).lhp.sdu.edu.cn
  • Historical Textuality: History as shaped by literary forms, such as poems influencing gardens or steles, blurring boundaries between literature and social life.sciencedirect.com
  • Shift to Literary Culture: From isolated "literature" to embedded "literary culture," using snapshots and moments (inspired by A New History of French Literature, 1989) to foster plural, non-linear explanations.sciencedirect.com

This influence appears in thematic framings, such as Owen's "Cultural Tang" analyzing post-An Lushan shifts, or Tian's integration of Buddhism and manuscript pragmatics, promoting contextual embedding to rectify anachronisms.tsla.researchcommons.org

4. Redefining "Chineseness"

The work redefines "Chinese literature" and "Chineseness" by pivoting from nation-state paradigms to "discursive communities"—fluid groups united by shared literary values, fashions, and networks, transcending political borders. This shift broadens the scope:tsla.researchcommons.org

  • Language Impacts: From ornate, parallel court styles to plain, individualized diction reflecting proto-professionalism and detachment from state ideology.cuhk.edu.hk
  • Geographical Impacts: Decentralization from imperial centers (e.g., Chang'an) to regional locales (e.g., Runzhou, Xiangyang), incorporating peripheral and multicultural elements like Dunhuang narratives with Buddhist influences.lhp.sdu.edu.cn

A concrete example is Chapter IV, "The Cultural Tang," which examines temple-visiting poems: early courtly examples (e.g., Xie Lingyun, Jiang Zong) with imperial symbolism evolve to mid-period regulated verses (Shen Quanqi, Song Zhiwen) and late ascetic works (Jia Dao's "bitter chanting" in decentralized circles), illustrating community-driven changes detached from court authority. This redefinition expands Chineseness to shared cultural prestige, including non-Han exchanges and diaspora writings, challenging Sinocentric boundaries.sciencedirect.comsciencedirect.com

In summary, these theoretical renovations in The Cambridge History offer a refreshed framework for understanding Chinese literature as a vibrant, contextualized cultural phenomenon.
